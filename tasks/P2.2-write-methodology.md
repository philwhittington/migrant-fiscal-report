**2.1 Data sources**

Describe the Hughes AN 26/02 dataset:
- What it is: administrative tax data from Inland Revenue, covering all individual taxpayers in NZ
- Time coverage: tax years 2000 to 2024
- Key variables: personal income tax paid, transaction category (resident/non-resident/other), visa subcategory (skilled, family, humanitarian, etc.), age band (10-year), sex, nationality
- What it captures: the revenue side of the fiscal equation -- how much tax migrants pay relative to NZ-born
- Important detail: tax values are presented in billions; the quantile distributions (p25, p50, p75) are in dollars per person per year
- Note the confidentialisation: cells with fewer than 6 taxpayers are suppressed (marked 'S')

Describe the Wright and Nguyen AN 24/09 dataset:
- What it is: a full fiscal incidence model for the 2018/19 fiscal year
- What it covers: both the revenue side (income tax, GST, excise, other tax) and the expenditure side (education, health, NZ Super, welfare benefits, other government spending)
- Key structure: net fiscal incidence by single year of age (0-100+), broken into approximately 20 revenue and expenditure categories
- Why it matters: this provides the expenditure template that Hughes does not contain
- Limitation: it reflects NZ-average patterns, not migrant-specific expenditure

**2.2 Matching methodology**

Explain how the two datasets are combined:
1. Hughes provides migrant-specific tax payments by visa type, age band, and year
2. Wright and Nguyen provides the full fiscal balance (revenue minus expenditure) for the NZ population by age
3. The matching substitutes the revenue side from Wright and Nguyen with actual migrant tax data from Hughes, keeping the expenditure side as a NZ-average template
4. Age band alignment: Hughes uses 10-year bands (20-29, 30-39, etc.); these are mapped to the single-year-of-age W&N data by averaging across the 10 constituent years
5. Year adjustment: Hughes 2024 tax data is deflated to 2018/19 dollars using CPI to match the W&N fiscal year (state the deflation factor used, read it from SCRATCHPAD.md or the processing scripts)
6. The result: for each visa type and arrival age, a year-by-year profile of net fiscal contribution from arrival to age 100

**2.3 The NPV formula**

Present the NPV formula clearly. Use this structure:

NPV = sum from t=0 to T of [ R(t) * S(t) / (1+r)^t ]

Where:
- t = years since arrival in NZ
- T = years from arrival to age 100
- R(t) = net fiscal contribution in year t (migrant tax paid minus NZ-average government expenditure for that age)
- S(t) = retention probability -- the probability the migrant is still in NZ at time t (from Hughes Table 14/16 survival curves)
- r = real discount rate (3.5%, consistent with Treasury's standard CBA guidance)

Explain each component:
- R(t) varies by age and visa type. For ages where migrants pay more tax than the NZ-average expenditure on their age group, R(t) is positive (fiscal surplus). For ages 65+, expenditure (NZ Super, health) typically exceeds tax revenue, so R(t) turns negative.
- S(t) captures out-migration. A migrant who arrives at 30 and leaves at 45 contributes for 15 years but never draws NZ Super. This is the key mechanism that makes migrant NPV higher than NZ-born NPV.
- The discount rate r reflects the social time preference. 3.5% is Treasury's standard rate for cost-benefit analysis.

**2.4 Key assumptions and limitations of the method**

List and explain each assumption:
1. **Discount rate**: 3.5% real, per Treasury CBA guidance. Sensitivity tested at 2% and 5%.
2. **NZ Super eligibility**: assumed eligible after 10 years of residence from age 20. Simplified -- does not model partial entitlements or bilateral social security agreements.
3. **Healthy migrant adjustment**: working-age migrants are assumed to use 85% of NZ-average health expenditure (reflecting the "healthy migrant effect" documented in NZ health literature). This narrows with age.
4. **Retention extrapolation**: Hughes provides actual retention data for the 2005 cohort out to 18 years. Beyond 18 years, retention is extrapolated using [state the method -- parametric curve, linear decay, etc., based on what the processing scripts actually did].
5. **Expenditure template**: uses NZ-average expenditure by age, not migrant-specific. Migrants may differ (e.g., lower welfare uptake in early years, different education patterns if children arrive mid-school).
6. **Cross-sectional, not longitudinal**: this is a cross-sectional snapshot approach. We observe what different cohorts look like at a point in time, not tracking the same individuals over their lifecycle.
7. **Year mismatch**: tax data is 2024; fiscal incidence data is 2018/19. CPI adjustment mitigates but does not eliminate this gap.

**2.5 What this analysis is not**

One short paragraph making clear:
- This is not individual-level microsimulation (it uses published summary statistics)
- This is not causal analysis (it does not estimate the causal effect of migration policy changes)
- This does not capture second-round effects (housing, infrastructure, wages, productivity spillovers)
- It is a proof of concept using public data, intended to motivate a full lifecycle model using linked administrative microdata (the Integrated Data Infrastructure)

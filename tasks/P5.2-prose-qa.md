# Task P5.2: Prose QA — final style review

## Objective

Review the report prose for compliance with Heuser|Whittington report writing standards and fix any issues. The report should read as a professional analytical document — precise, evidence-based, and free of advocacy or vague language.

## Inputs

- `content/migrant-fiscal-impact.md` — the report markdown

## Expected outputs

- `content/migrant-fiscal-impact.md` — updated with any prose fixes
- `.agent/qa-prose-report.md` — a QA report documenting all findings and fixes

## Execution instructions

### Step 1: Read the full report

Read `content/migrant-fiscal-impact.md` from start to finish, paying attention to structure, tone, and precision.

### Step 2: Check heading style

All headings must use sentence case (first word capitalised, rest lowercase unless proper nouns).

- CORRECT: "The fiscal impact of migration to New Zealand"
- CORRECT: "Income convergence by nationality"
- WRONG: "The Fiscal Impact Of Migration To New Zealand"
- WRONG: "INCOME CONVERGENCE BY NATIONALITY"

Check every heading (lines starting with #, ##, ###, etc.) and fix any that violate sentence case.

### Step 3: Check for vague qualifiers

Search for and flag/fix these vague terms:
- "significant" / "significantly" — replace with the actual magnitude ("15 percentage points higher" not "significantly higher")
- "substantial" — replace with specific quantity
- "considerable" — replace with specific quantity
- "some" — replace with approximate number or percentage
- "many" — replace with approximate number or percentage
- "most" — replace with percentage or "more than X%"
- "very" — usually delete entirely
- "quite" — usually delete entirely
- "fairly" — usually delete entirely
- "somewhat" — replace with specific qualifier

Exception: these are acceptable when the underlying data genuinely does not support precision, but in that case add a caveat explaining why.

### Step 4: Check source citations

Every numerical claim should cite its source. The acceptable forms are:
- Parenthetical: "(Hughes 2024, Table 5)"
- Footnote reference: "[^1]"
- Inline: "According to Hughes (2024)..."

Check that:
- Every number has a citation
- Citations reference real tables/figures (Table 1-8 from Hughes, or the analysis scripts)
- No "orphan citations" (citations that do not match any reference in the bibliography)

### Step 5: Check for advocacy

The report should present evidence, not advocate for policy positions. Flag and fix:
- "New Zealand should..." -> rewrite as "The evidence suggests..." or "This finding implies..."
- "It is essential that..." -> rewrite as "The data indicates..."
- "We recommend..." -> acceptable if clearly labelled as the author's professional assessment
- "The government must..." -> rewrite as "The analysis points to..."
- Any normative language that goes beyond what the evidence supports

### Step 6: Check hedging

Appropriate hedging examples:
- "The data suggest that..." (not "The data prove that...")
- "This estimate is subject to..." (acknowledge uncertainty)
- "Based on the available data..." (acknowledge limitations)

But avoid over-hedging:
- "It might possibly be the case that perhaps..." — too hedged
- Just state the finding and its confidence level clearly

### Step 7: Check voice and paragraph length

- Prefer active voice: "Skilled migrants contribute $X" not "A contribution of $X is made by skilled migrants"
- Flag any paragraphs longer than 6 sentences — they should be split
- Flag any sentences longer than ~40 words — they should be simplified

### Step 8: Check section transitions

Between major sections, there should be a connecting sentence or paragraph that:
- Summarises the key finding of the section just completed
- Introduces what the next section will cover
- Maintains narrative flow

Check especially between:
- Executive summary -> first body section
- Each major analytical section -> the next
- Final body section -> methodology
- Methodology -> references

### Step 9: Check executive summary consistency

Read the executive summary and compare each claim against the corresponding section in the body:
- Does the executive summary accurately reflect the body findings?
- Are the numbers consistent?
- Does the executive summary introduce any claims not supported in the body?
